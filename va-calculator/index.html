Understanding Veterans Affairs Disability Math

The VA uses the "whole person" concept. Instead of adding disabilities together (50% + 30% = 80%), each disability is calculated based on remaining capacity.

Example Calculation:

First disability: 50% (leaves you 50% able)

Second disability: 30% of remaining 50% = 15%

Combined: 50% + 15% = 65% (rounds to 70% for pay)

Frequently Asked Questions

How does VA math work? ▼

VA math uses the "whole person" concept. Each disability is calculated as a percentage of your remaining ability. For example, if you have a 50% disability, you're 50% able. A second 30% disability is calculated as 30% of the remaining 50%, which equals 15%. Your combined rating would be 50% + 15% = 65%, which rounds to 70% for payment purposes.

Why doesn't 50% + 50% = 100%? ▼

Because VA math considers your remaining ability. If you have a 50% disability, you're 50% able. A second 50% disability is calculated as 50% of the remaining 50%, which equals 25%. Your combined rating would be 50% + 25% = 75%, which rounds to 80% for payment purposes.

What is the bilateral factor? ▼

The bilateral factor applies when you have disabilities affecting both sides of paired body parts (both arms, both legs, or paired skeletal muscles). The VA adds 10% of the combined value of these bilateral disabilities before combining with other disabilities. For example, if you have 30% for your left knee and 20% for your right knee, the bilateral factor would add 10% of their combined value (which is 44%), giving you an additional 4.4% before rounding. This recognizes that bilateral disabilities are more disabling than the sum of individual ratings.

Can I receive more than 100% disability? ▼

No, the maximum combined rating is 100%. However, you can receive additional compensation for dependents, Special Monthly Compensation (SMC), and other benefits that can increase your total monthly payment beyond the base 100% rate.

How often do VA disability rates increase? ▼

VA disability rates typically increase annually based on the cost-of-living adjustment (COLA). The rates are usually announced in December and take effect on January 1st of the following year.

How do I file for VA disability? ▼

You can file for VA disability benefits online at VA.gov, by mail, or in person at a VA regional office. You'll need medical evidence connecting your condition to your military service, along with your DD-214 and other supporting documents.

Veterans Affairs Disability Rating Guide

The VA assigns disability ratings based on the severity of your service-connected condition. Ratings are given in 10% increments from 0% to 100%.

0% Rating: Service-connected condition exists but doesn't cause significant impairment
10%-20%: Mild impairment with some impact on daily activities
30%-40%: Moderate impairment affecting work and daily life
50%-70%: Significant impairment with substantial impact on employment
80%-90%: Severe impairment with major limitations
100%: Total disability or unemployability due to service-connected conditions

Additional Veterans Affairs Benefits

Beyond basic disability compensation, veterans may qualify for additional benefits:

Special Monthly Compensation (SMC)

Additional compensation for veterans with severe disabilities requiring special care, loss of use of limbs, or other qualifying conditions.

Aid & Attendance

Additional monthly payment for veterans who need help with daily activities or are housebound due to service-connected disabilities.

Individual Unemployability (IU)

May qualify for 100% compensation if your service-connected disabilities prevent you from maintaining gainful employment.

Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC)

Monthly benefits for surviving spouses and dependent children of veterans who died from service-connected conditions.
